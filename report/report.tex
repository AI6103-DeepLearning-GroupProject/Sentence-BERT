\begin{abstract}
This project revisits the original Sentence-BERT (SBERT) recipe on the legacy
sentence-transformers v0.2.4 codebase (Dec 2019) and evaluates reproducibility under a Slurm HPC environment.
Our practical objective is to identify low-compute improvements that remain compatible with a 2019-era training stack.
We first establish a reproducible baseline around BERT-based SBERT training and STSBenchmark evaluation,
then prepare an extensible runtime configuration for controlled comparison across model variants and data curricula.
This report summarizes the current pipeline and preliminary results.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Sentence-BERT \cite{reimers-gurevych-2019-sentence-bert} addresses the inefficiency of cross-encoder sentence-pair
inference by learning fixed-size sentence embeddings that can be compared with simple similarity functions.
This design dramatically reduces pairwise search cost while retaining strong semantic textual similarity (STS) performance.

In this course project, we reproduce and extend the early SBERT setting using the original-style implementation
on sentence-transformers v0.2.4 (Dec 2019). The engineering target is not to maximize raw leaderboard performance with modern
large-scale pretraining, but to obtain measurable gains under constrained compute and reproducible Slurm execution.

Our current milestone focuses on:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Building robust Slurm scripts for data setup, training, and evaluation.
    \item Unifying runtime options via YAML configuration.
    \item Establishing a stable baseline before testing enhanced training objectives.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Works}
The core reference is Sentence-BERT \cite{reimers-gurevych-2019-sentence-bert},
which adapts BERT \cite{devlin-etal-2019-bert} into a siamese/triplet framework for efficient
sentence embedding learning. The original paper reports strong transfer performance on STS tasks
with dramatically faster semantic search compared with vanilla BERT pairwise inference.

Our implementation scope currently follows the original SBERT spirit:
NLI/STS-driven supervised fine-tuning, pooling-based sentence embeddings, and STSBenchmark-based reporting.
The extension path in this project emphasizes lightweight training-strategy upgrades (e.g., multi-stage curricula)
that can be integrated into the legacy codebase with low engineering overhead.

\section{Experiment Setups}
\textbf{Codebase.}
We use a legacy Sentence-BERT repository aligned with sentence-transformers v0.2.4 (2019-style workflow).

\textbf{Execution environment.}
All experiments are executed using Single GPU NVIDIA L40S.

\textbf{Dataset protocol.}
For STSBenchmark, we strictly follow the standard split protocol:
\texttt{sts-train.csv} is used for training, \texttt{sts-dev.csv} for model selection, and
\texttt{sts-test.csv} only for final reporting. This separation is preserved in all runtime modes,
so the final cosine Spearman numbers are evaluated without data leakage from the test split.
Training/evaluation scripts are configured to normalize expected dataset paths and optionally auto-download
required files when missing.

\textbf{Metrics.}
We report Cosine Spearman correlations under similarities, as this is the one used and promoted in the original SBERT work.
For multi-seed evaluation, we aggregate mean and standard deviation over repeated runs.

\section{Methodology}
Our goal is to improve the original SBERT recipe without breaking compatibility with the 2019
\texttt{sentence-transformers} training stack. All reported best runs used the \texttt{bert-base-uncased} 
with mean pooling and maximum sequence length 128,
while the training curriculum is switched through \texttt{config.yaml}. 
The common backbone of all experiments is a three-stage view of sentence embedding learning:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}(x)=\operatorname{MeanPool}(\operatorname{BERT}(x))
\end{equation}
We first warm-start the encoder on NLI, then optionally reshape the embedding geometry with an
intermediate objective, and finally adapt to STS with direct similarity supervision.

\paragraph{Baseline SBERT curriculum.}
The baseline \texttt{sts\_nli} mode follows the original SBERT intuition
\cite{reimers-gurevych-2019-sentence-bert}: NLI classification teaches coarse semantic relations,
while STS fine-tuning calibrates the embedding space for graded similarity. In the NLI stage, the
model is trained with the original SBERT sentence-pair classifier, where the two sentence embeddings
$\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are combined as the concatenation
$[\mathbf{u};\mathbf{v};|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}|]$ and passed to a softmax classification head. This
design is one of the key contributions of SBERT, because it transfers BERT into a siamese sentence
embedding framework while still exploiting strong supervised NLI signals. In the final STS stage, we
train on \texttt{sts-train.csv}, select by \texttt{sts-dev.csv}, and report only on
\texttt{sts-test.csv}; the objective is cosine regression on normalized STS labels:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{STS}}=\operatorname{MSE}\left(\cos(\mathbf{h}_i,\mathbf{h}_j), y_{ij}\right), \quad y_{ij}\in[0,1]
\end{equation}
This baseline is therefore not a trivial reference point, but a strong and carefully designed study
that remains the foundation for our later enhancements.

\paragraph{Enhancement I: AoE training between NLI and STS.}
The first high-performing branch inserts an AoE-style intermediate stage after NLI pretraining and
before STS fine-tuning. The motivation comes from recent work on angle-optimized embeddings
\cite{li-li-2024-aoe}: cosine similarity is easy to optimize when pairs are moderately similar, but
its gradient becomes weak in saturation regions close to $1$ or $-1$, which is undesirable for STS
where many pairs differ only subtly. The key idea is therefore not a different encoder architecture,
but optimizing \emph{angular ordering}: sentence pairs with higher semantic relatedness should receive
smaller relative angles. In our implementation this becomes a pairwise angle-ranking objective. For a pair
$(x_i,x_j)$ with embeddings $\mathbf{h}_i,\mathbf{h}_j$, the angle is
\begin{equation}
\Delta\theta_{ij}=
\operatorname{atan2}\left(
\begin{aligned}
&\left|\langle \mathbf{h}_i^{\Im}, \mathbf{h}_j^{\Re}\rangle
- \langle \mathbf{h}_i^{\Re}, \mathbf{h}_j^{\Im}\rangle\right|, \\
&\langle \mathbf{h}_i^{\Re}, \mathbf{h}_j^{\Re}\rangle
+ \langle \mathbf{h}_i^{\Im}, \mathbf{h}_j^{\Im}\rangle + \epsilon
\end{aligned}
\right)
\end{equation}
The model then applies a pairwise ranking loss so that sentence pairs with higher supervision scores
obtain smaller angles:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{angle}}=
\log \left(1+\sum_{y_p>y_q}\exp\left(\frac{\Delta\theta_p-\Delta\theta_q}{\tau_a}\right)\right)
\end{equation}
The real/imaginary split is only the implementation mechanism used to compute this angular relation;
the methodological focus is the ranking of pairwise angles rather than a ``complex-valued'' model in
itself.

The supervision for this stage is built from both AllNLI and STSBenchmark. 
We first maps NLI labels to graded scores
(\texttt{entailment}$=1.0$, \texttt{neutral}$=0.5$, \texttt{contradiction}$=0.0$), normalizes STS
scores to $[0,1]$, merges the two sources, and deduplicates unordered pairs by keeping the maximum
score. This gives AoE a denser ordinal training signal than binary similarity learning. Our code
also adds a symmetric in-batch contrastive term:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{AoE}}=\lambda_{\text{angle}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{angle}}+\lambda_{\text{cl}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{cl}}
\end{equation}
Intuitively, the angle loss improves local ranking sensitivity for fine-grained semantic similarity,
while the auxiliary contrastive term keeps positive pairs tightly aligned. After this geometry-aware
stage, we still perform the same final STS cosine fine-tuning for a fair comparison with the baseline.

\paragraph{Enhancement II: MNRL with UniReg before STS.}
The second best branch replaces the intermediate stage with hard-negative contrastive (from NLI) learning plus a
uniformity regularizer. The contrastive component is Multiple Negatives Ranking Loss (MNRL), which
can be viewed as a sentence-pair instantiation of the InfoNCE objective
\cite{rusak2025infonce}. In our setting, the matched positive is contrasted against all other
candidates in the batch together with optional hard negatives, making it closely related to the
supervised SimCSE formulation \cite{gao-etal-2021-simcse}. For each anchor sentence, its paired
positive is treated as the only correct target, while all other positives in the same batch and
explicit contradiction examples are used as negatives:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{MNRL}}=
-\log\frac{\exp(\operatorname{sim}(\mathbf{z}_i,\mathbf{z}_i^+)/\tau)}
{\sum_{k}\exp(\operatorname{sim}(\mathbf{z}_i,\mathbf{c}_k)/\tau)}
\end{equation}
Here $\mathbf{z}$ denotes L2-normalized sentence embeddings and $\mathbf{c}_k$ denotes the candidate
pool formed by the matched positive together with in-batch and hard-negative examples. In our data
builder, NLI entailment pairs are used as positives and contradiction hypotheses for the same premise
are sampled as hard negatives, matching the supervised contrastive practice advocated by SimCSE.

While studying world models, we noticed the SIGReg regularizer used in LeWM \cite{maes2026lewm},
which prompted us to ask whether SBERT embeddings may also suffer from a related dimensional
collapse or cone effect; this heuristic observation led us to look for a lightweight geometric
regularizer and eventually adopt UniReg.
Following the alignment-uniformity perspective of contrastive representation learning
\cite{wang-isola-2020-alignment-uniformity}, we add a weak hyperspherical uniformity regularizer:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{uni}}=
\log\left(\frac{1}{|\mathcal{P}|}\sum_{u\neq v}\exp\left(-t\left\|\mathbf{z}_u-\mathbf{z}_v\right\|_2^2\right)+\epsilon\right),
\end{equation}
and optimize
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{MNRL-UniReg}}=\mathcal{L}_{\text{MNRL}}+\lambda_{\text{uni}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{uni}}
\end{equation}
Minimizing this term encourages embeddings to spread more evenly on the unit hypersphere, which
reduces representation collapse and improves global geometry, while MNRL still preserves strong
positive-pair alignment. In our configuration, UniReg is intentionally lightweight
($\lambda_{\text{uni}}=10^{-3}$), so it acts as a global geometric prior rather than overriding the
task loss.

\paragraph{Why these two enhancements are complementary.}
Although both branches improve cosine Spearman on STSBenchmark, they intervene at different levels of
the embedding geometry. AoE mainly changes the \emph{local ranking signal} by replacing saturated
cosine supervision with angle-based ordering on graded pairs. MNRL+UniReg instead keeps the familiar
contrastive objective but improves the \emph{global distribution} of embeddings through hard negatives
and hyperspherical spreading. This contrast makes the two methods a useful pair of lightweight
upgrades for a legacy SBERT codebase: one sharpens fine-grained similarity ordering, while the other
stabilizes the space in which retrieval-style similarity is computed.
You may ask: Why not put them together? We did try a joint \texttt{MNRL+AoE} stage, and will be anylysed in the case study section.

\section{Results and Analysis}
Table~\ref{tab:sts-benchmark-results} summarizes all completed STSBenchmark experiments under the cosine
Spearman metric, sorted by the final mean score. Following Sentence-BERT
\cite{reimers-gurevych-2019-sentence-bert}, we use cosine Spearman as the primary metric because it
better reflects ranking quality for semantic similarity than Pearson correlation.

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Main STSBenchmark results for the methods proposed in this project, sorted by cosine Spearman mean.}
\label{tab:sts-benchmark-results}
\resizebox{\columnwidth}{!}{
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\hline
Setting & Runs & Cosine Spearman (\%) \\
\hline
\texttt{STS} & 3 & $84.854 \pm 0.394$ \\
\texttt{NLI+STS} & 3 & $84.853 \pm 0.474$ \\
\texttt{NLI+AoE+STS} & 3 & $86.658 \pm 0.278$ \\
\texttt{NLI+MNRL+STS} & 3 & $86.749 \pm 0.127$ \\
\texttt{NLI+MNRL-UniReg+STS} & 3 & \textbf{$86.792 \pm 0.126$} \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
}
\end{table}

Three observations are clear. First, all three best-performing settings insert an intermediate
embedding-geometry refinement stage between NLI and final STS fine-tuning, and all of them improve
substantially over the plain \texttt{STS} and \texttt{NLI+STS} baselines. Second, the strongest result
is obtained by \texttt{NLI+MNRL+UniReg+STS}, whose cosine Spearman reaches $86.792\%$, slightly
exceeding the rerun \texttt{NLI+MNRL+STS} result of $86.749\%$ and the AoE branch at $86.658\%$.
This suggests that adding a weak global uniformity prior is more consistently beneficial than using
contrastive learning alone.

Relative to the best baseline (\texttt{STS}, $84.854\%$), the gains are approximately
$+1.94$ points for UniReg, $+1.90$ for rerun MNRL, and $+1.80$ for AoE. Therefore, the current
evidence favors lightweight geometric regularization and ranking-aware intermediate training as the
most promising directions in this legacy SBERT setting.

\section{Case Study}

Not all design changes improve performance, even when they appear intuitively compatible with SBERT.
We first highlight two failed method variants on STSBenchmark itself, then discuss the broader
transfer failures of MNRL on retrieval and SentEval.

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Failure cases on STSBenchmark for additional design variants.}
\label{tab:failure-sts-methods}
\resizebox{\columnwidth}{!}{
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\hline
Setting & Runs & Cosine Spearman (\%) \\
\hline
Baseline:\texttt{NLI+STS} & 3 & $84.853 \pm 0.474$ \\
\texttt{NLI+MNRL+AoE+STS} & 3 & $85.667 \pm 0.002$ \\
\texttt{NLI+MNRL+STS(CoSENT)} & 1 & $85.747 \pm 0.000$ \\
\texttt{NLI+STS+attn. pooling} & 3 & $84.732 \pm 0.814$ \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
}
\end{table}

These two results are informative because both variants add modeling complexity without improving the
main metric. The joint \texttt{MNRL+AoE} setup is much weaker than standalone \texttt{MNRL} or AoE,
suggesting that local angle ranking and contrastive geometry shaping can interfere when optimized
together in a naive multi-objective stage. The attention-pooling variant also fails to beat even the
plain \texttt{NLI+STS} baseline, which suggests that changing the pooling architecture is less useful
than improving the training objective under our current data and compute budget.
